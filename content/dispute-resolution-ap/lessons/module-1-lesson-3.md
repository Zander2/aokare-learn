# Stakeholders, Roles, and Communication Flows
## Module 1: Foundations of AP Dispute Resolution | Lesson 3

**Learning Objectives:**
- Map the internal and external stakeholders involved in a typical AP dispute
- Describe the decision authority at each escalation tier
- Outline the communication protocols between AP, procurement, receiving, and suppliers

---

## Why Stakeholder Mapping Matters

A dispute is not resolved by a single person. It moves through a network of people, each with different information, different authority, and different incentives. The AP clerk wants the invoice cleared from their queue. The procurement manager wants to protect the supplier relationship. The supplier's AR team wants to get paid. The finance controller wants accurate books.

If you do not understand who is involved and what each party can do, disputes stall. They stall in inboxes, in approval queues, and in the gap between departments that each assume someone else is handling it. Mapping stakeholders is not an academic exercise — it is the blueprint for building workflows that actually move disputes to closure.

## Internal Stakeholders

### AP Clerk / AP Specialist

**Role in disputes:** First-line detection and documentation. The AP clerk typically runs the three-way match (or reviews automated match results), identifies discrepancies, and initiates the dispute process.

**Decision authority:** Can resolve discrepancies within tolerance thresholds. Can approve adjustments under a defined dollar limit (commonly $500-$2,000 depending on the organization). Cannot write off disputed amounts or approve payments that exceed PO values without manager approval.

**Information they have:** Invoice details, PO data, GRN data, vendor master records, payment history.

**What they need from others:** Confirmation from receiving about what actually arrived. Confirmation from procurement about contract terms. Authorization from management for resolutions above their limit.

### AP Manager / AP Supervisor

**Role in disputes:** Second-tier decision-maker. Reviews disputes that the AP clerk cannot resolve within their authority. Approves or rejects proposed resolutions. Manages the overall dispute queue and ensures SLAs are met.

**Decision authority:** Can approve adjustments and write-offs within a higher dollar limit (commonly $5,000-$25,000). Can authorize payment holds. Can escalate to the finance controller or procurement director.

**Information they have:** Aggregate dispute data, vendor performance history, team workload and capacity.

**What they need from others:** Detailed discrepancy documentation from AP clerks. Supplier relationship context from procurement. Budget impact analysis from finance.

### Procurement / Purchasing

**Role in disputes:** Provides contract and relationship context. Procurement knows the terms that were negotiated, the history with the vendor, and the strategic importance of the supplier relationship.

**Decision authority:** Can confirm or deny that a price change was agreed upon. Can negotiate with the supplier on contract terms. Can recommend whether to push hard on a dispute or accept a compromise to preserve the relationship.

**Information they have:** Contract terms, pricing agreements, vendor scorecards, market intelligence, negotiation history.

**What they need from others:** Specific discrepancy details from AP. Volume and frequency data to assess whether this is a one-off or a pattern.

### Warehouse / Receiving

**Role in disputes:** Provides ground truth about what was physically received. They are the source of GRN data and can confirm or clarify quantity and quality issues.

**Decision authority:** Can confirm receipt quantities, document damages, and provide inspection results. Cannot make financial decisions about the dispute.

**Information they have:** GRN records, damage reports, inspection results, delivery timestamps, photos of damaged goods.

**What they need from others:** Clarity on what the dispute is about so they know what to investigate. PO details to confirm what was expected.

### Finance Controller / CFO Office

**Role in disputes:** Highest internal escalation point. Involved in high-value disputes, systemic issues, and disputes with strategic suppliers that could affect broader business relationships.

**Decision authority:** Can approve write-offs above the AP manager's limit. Can authorize legal action. Can make final decisions on payment holds that affect cash flow.

**Information they have:** Cash flow forecasts, budget impact, audit requirements, board reporting obligations.

**What they need from others:** A complete case file with all evidence, a clear recommendation, and an assessment of the financial and relationship impact of each resolution option.

## External Stakeholders

### Supplier AR Team

**Role:** The supplier's mirror of your AP team. They receive dispute notifications, investigate on their end, and process credit notes or revised invoices.

**What they care about:** Getting paid. Closing open items on their books. Meeting their own internal metrics for DSO (Days Sales Outstanding) and AR aging.

**Communication style:** Typically formal and structured when dealing with large buyers. May be less responsive when the disputed amount is small relative to their total business with you.

### Supplier Sales Representative

**Role:** The commercial relationship owner. Gets involved when a dispute threatens the relationship or when the AR team cannot resolve it independently.

**What they care about:** Maintaining the account. Protecting their commission. Keeping the buyer happy enough to continue ordering.

**When they get involved:** When the dispute drags past 30 days. When the buyer threatens to withhold payment on undisputed invoices. When the AP teams on both sides reach an impasse.

### Third-Party Mediators

**Role:** External parties brought in when direct negotiation fails. Can include industry mediators, arbitration services, or legal counsel.

**When they get involved:** Rarely — most AP disputes are resolved bilaterally. Third parties enter the picture for high-value disputes ($100,000+), disputes involving potential fraud, or disputes where the business relationship has broken down.

## Escalation Tiers

A clear escalation structure prevents disputes from stalling. Here is a typical four-tier model:

### Tier 1: AP Clerk to Supplier AR (Days 1-7)

The AP clerk contacts the supplier's AR team directly. This is where 50-65% of disputes should be resolved. The communication is transactional: here is the discrepancy, here is the evidence, please issue a credit note or clarify.

**Trigger for escalation:** No response within 5 business days, or supplier rejects the dispute without adequate counter-evidence.

### Tier 2: AP Manager to Supplier AR Manager (Days 7-14)

The AP manager escalates the dispute to the supplier's AR manager. Both sides review the evidence. This is where most negotiation happens — compromise solutions like splitting the difference, accepting a partial credit, or adjusting future pricing.

**Trigger for escalation:** Amount exceeds AP manager's authority. Supplier AR manager is unresponsive. Dispute involves contract interpretation that requires procurement input.

### Tier 3: Procurement + Finance to Supplier Sales/Management (Days 14-30)

Procurement and finance leadership engage with the supplier's commercial team. At this level, the dispute is evaluated in the context of the broader relationship: total spend, contract renewal timing, alternative supplier availability.

**Trigger for escalation:** Dispute value exceeds $25,000. Resolution requires contract amendment. Supplier alleges fraud or bad faith.

### Tier 4: Legal / Executive (Days 30+)

Legal counsel and executive leadership get involved. This tier is expensive, slow, and often relationship-ending. It is the option of last resort.

**When used:** Fraud allegations. Breach of contract claims. Disputes exceeding $100,000. Regulatory implications.

## Communication Protocols

### Channel Selection

| Dispute Type | Preferred Channel | Why |
|-------------|-------------------|-----|
| Standard pricing/quantity | Supplier portal or structured email | Creates audit trail, allows attachment of evidence |
| Urgent (payment hold affecting supplier operations) | Phone + follow-up email | Speed, but documented |
| Complex (multiple line items, contract questions) | Scheduled call + shared document | Needs real-time discussion with shared reference |
| Escalated | Formal letter / executive email | Signals seriousness, creates legal-grade documentation |

### SLAs for Communication

Every organization should define internal and external SLAs:

- **Internal handoff:** When AP needs information from receiving or procurement, the response should come within 2 business days. Disputes stall most often at internal handoff points, not in supplier communication.
- **Supplier notification:** The supplier should be notified within 3 business days of dispute detection. Delayed notification weakens your position and frustrates the supplier.
- **Supplier response:** Expect a substantive response within 5-7 business days. Set this expectation in your supplier onboarding and terms of trade.
- **Follow-up cadence:** If no response, follow up at day 7, day 14, and day 21 with increasing urgency.

### Documentation at Each Handoff

Every time a dispute moves from one person or team to another, the handoff must include:

1. **Dispute summary:** One paragraph stating what the dispute is about
2. **Current status:** What has been done, what response has been received
3. **Evidence package:** All supporting documents (PO, GRN, invoice, correspondence)
4. **Recommended action:** What the person handing off thinks should happen next
5. **Deadline:** When a decision or action is needed

Without this structure, the next person spends 30-60 minutes reconstructing context that the previous person already had. Multiply that by 4 handoffs and you have added half a day of labor to every dispute.

## The Cost of Misaligned Stakeholders

When stakeholders are not aligned, disputes become political. Procurement does not want AP to antagonize a strategic supplier. AP does not want procurement to override a legitimate discrepancy. Finance wants everything resolved by month-end. Receiving does not want to be blamed for a GRN error.

The solution is a documented dispute policy that specifies:
- Who has authority to make what decisions
- What evidence is required at each stage
- What SLAs apply to each handoff
- How conflicts between departments are resolved (typically by the finance controller or a designated dispute committee)

This policy should be reviewed annually and updated when organizational structures change.
